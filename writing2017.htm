JOSE NUNEZ

This is the way of the mute,

to hit a perfect note,

a beautiful melody that never felt so real.

I can’t hear it,

but the one who created it

made me a prisoner of his art.

I feel what he felt.

I can be deaf and hear every emotion.

Talk about speaking your mind,

do you want to hear ours?

We are writing.

EDDIE ARROYO

                        

How does the world see me? Where do I belong? The world sees me as a New York Rican who did not finish his education. I made a lot of mistakes when I was young. I was raised as a street kid. I learned if I kept going the same way I would end up dead or upstate in jail.

Where do I belong?

When I was twenty years old I got shot in the head. I had a two-month-old child. Now I have five children, four boys and one girl. The two month old is now sixteen. I have a fourteen year old, a thirteen year old, a ten year old, and a seven year old. My daughter is thirteen.

Where do I belong?

I know I belong with my five children to teach them about life and my own mistakes. I am a father.

Where I am from

at night it is a beautiful look,

but it’s a look in disguise.

Where I am from the projects are smelly.

Where I am from

you can see the East River, the Hudson,

and the FDR Drive.

Where I am from

you can see rich and poor.

Where I am from you try to make it anywhere.

Where do I belong?

The gift I want to give my children is me. I want to be by their sides because I know they need me. I always wanted my father. He went to jail when I was little. I was three. When he came out somebody shot and killed him I was seven. I don’t want my children to grow up without a father. They are my gift, and I want to be their gift.

Dear Kids,

I hope you guys are doing great in school. I know things have changed these past couple of months because daddy is gone. Please don’t act up daddy will be out soon. You guys do the right things; this is not a place for anyone. Just remember what I told you guys, education is the key to success and to work.

I am sorry for my mistakes and for not having been by your side during the holidays and for not being there when you get out of school. I’m in jail where it is not cool to be. I know you guys miss me because I miss you. It is good when you guys visit me. You can go back home. I can’t. Please learn from daddy’s mistakes.

Love,

Daddy

NICHOLAS DARLING

I don't write much. I don’t feel like it’s worth my time to write something down when I know no one will read it or care about it. I have much to say, but I don’t know where to start. I feel like I wouldn’t put a stray dog in my place, imprisonment and feeling I am losing my sanity. Perhaps freedom is sanity.

Is freedom sanity?

I’m just a number here, and outside I have never made much of myself. I have to face the mistakes that left me where I am, rock bottom, broke, and homeless.

I know what the world expects of me, to get out only to start that cycle that many people know so well, spend half of my life, if not more, incarcerated, to rot away never realizing my potential.

To be honest I am quite scared, afraid I won’t be able to make it while staying a good, honest person. From my experience “Good Guys Finish Last” seems to be true. I see bad people getting what they want by walking over good people while I am wondering where is Karma. Is our world really this type of place?

Even while I am down though I see the good in others in this jail, an unlikely place. I have had many people help me out, and thanks to them I have the strength to try to be a better man.

I am rock bottom, broke, and homeless.

JOSEPH L. FRANCIS

We as incarcerated men do not have solo rights to anything anymore, not even our own crazy lives. The power of our expression plays an important role for those of us who are incarcerated in our community. There is a difference in art as an expression of an individual and art as an expression of a culture. Is one more valuable than the other?

We are writing.

Today I sit here in the belly of the beast, Monroe Correctional Facility, serving nine months of a year back to back. I think about that day and the million and one ways my being here could have been avoided. I think about what could have prevented this nightmare. What I have come to learn is that in every situation there is time for consequential thinking. Every action has consequences, good or bad. Every time we make a decision there are results.

I have made some horrible mistakes. I am human. Human beings make mistakes and mess up decisions. I apologize for all of the bad decisions I have made under the influence of drugs and alcohol that got me here. I’ve learned a lot of things.

I regret a lot of things I done under the influence of drugs and alcohol.

To my grandkids, please know that you are wise and intelligent young men. I apologize to you for my ways and actions. I will try to be a better person for your sakes and for our family and friends.

To me education is a teaching and learning process and the key to some or all of the doors of success. Knowledge can be split into two words, know and ledge. Know yourself before you fall off the edge. You are the edge, and education will keep you away from walls and bars. Knowledge has no ending.

A stunning proportion of Black men in the United States, fully thirty-five percent (http://abcnews.go.com/Politics/story?id=121724 ), cannot vote because they have been convicted of felonies.

Where I’m from I stand in the streets without money

and nobody knows my name

and nobody cares.

When I speak with my soul, the door closes.

In the county jail with all of my crazy passion I suffer my disgrace.

Where I’m from there are choices you have to make,

not once

but every time they come up.

Is the violent poetry of the times

written in the blood of youth

or the blood of elders?

Where I’m from we have the right to lie

but not about the heart.

Where I’m from my task is to make you hear

because hearing is an art

that will make you feel

and above all make you see.

That is all, and that is everything.

Where I’m from go ahead kill us. We are already dead.

I glimpse in the distance

certain streets clearing silently in the morning

after the night’s demons have fled.

The system is broken. When I am released from these mental and physical shackles, I am going to tell my grandkids and nephews and nieces that yes minds do matter especially those of Black incarcerated men who cannot vote due to a felony conviction. I want to tell them about how incarceration has changed my life, and without any form of education we are realizing the revolving door scenario.

I would like to be able to look back and know that I have saved a young life from being a statistic. We who are incarcerated want to do something with our lives and not go in and out of a penitentiary. The United States has the highest rate of incarceration. Russia is second. We need to reduce the incarceration rate and spend money on education for inmates.

Being incarcerated makes me invisible, and the whole time I am in plain sight.

These walls and fences

are designed

for family and friends

to forget

you are still alive.

I hide my feelings and cry mentally. Being confined teaches me and others how to hide, or should I say disguise, our inner feelings. There’s a saying that goes like this, “A man with pride can be crushed more that a man without pride.”

I am a young man with the disease formally know as depression, and when I am listening to my radio and looking at my grandkids’ photo depression kicks in, and I begin to shed invisible tears. My pride won’t let me shed physical tears.

I

hide my tears

because eyes are watching.

I

wear the mask

and hide my face from others

so the world will not know who I am,

but

I can’t hide from myself.

Langston Hughes wrote very well about freedom. I can relate to what he wrote. Sometimes I feel as though I am also that rock that so many have stumped their toes on.

I love writing because I am free to express what I feel without being scrutinized but being understood for the things I have seen and done.

Writing

is a cloud

that I float on

until

I am brought down

by reality.

My great mistake is being here. What is the meaning of life? What is my purpose? What happened to my values? Why did I let outside influences decide what was good for me and how to life my life and what I needed or didn’t need to do?

I

hide my tears.

Sadness and sorrow and pain are so hard to express. Is the only way for us to express our inner feelings and thoughts by writing a song or a poem?

After reading our writing is it hard for you to believe that some of us have only seventh grade educations? Our writing makes me think about education and educating myself and others. Our writing gives me inspiration to continue my own education

FRANCISCO NEGRON

                        

Because of my background and past I get passed by and judged by my family and friends. Because of this I feel like breaking down, but to me showing that side of my feelings is a sign of weakness and where I come from we just don't do that. I never liked letting people see that side of me. I became a master of how to hide my feelings. People think I am always happy, but really I am a man who is depressed inside and no one will ever know because I have too much pride to let anyone see me cry.

I just keep standing strong.

Where I’m from my family and I were faced with many difficult situations that forced me to grow up fast.

Where I’m from there are many times that children have to take on responsibilities of an adult.

Where I’m from single mothers have no choice but to raise their children by themselves.

Where I’m from children may lose their parents to the evils of the street or the solitude of prison.

Where I’m from my mother did everything in her power to make sure her kids had what we needed to survive.

Where I’m from I had to take on the challenges of raising my little sister who has Downs Syndrome.

Where are you from?

ERIC BROWNLEE

I have learned that being locked up not only affects me, but it has a rippling effect. My incarceration has affected my mom, my dad, my sister, my brother, my nieces, my nephew, my girlfriend, and my community. My incarceration has taught me that my actions not only impact me, but they impact everyone around me and those who are unseen to me in some way, shape, or form.

As I was being herded to the mess hall in places like Elmira, Attica, and Comstock, I couldn't help but think I was no longer a person but instead a commodity, a way in which people are employed due to my incarceration. From the drivers who transport me from one facility to another, to the nurse I see at sick call, to the person who fills my order at commissary, to the officer who counts me at bedtime to make sure I have not escaped.

“Occupational Employment and Wages, May 2016

Includes deputy sheriffs and police who spend the majority of their time guarding prisoners in correctional institutions.

Employment NYS:        34,460

How has incarceration changed me? It has made me more self aware, more responsible, and more accepting of self-change.

I see myself today as a person who is the product of extremely dark places, a person who is spite of unfortunate circumstances and experiences has come full circle and finds light in spite of the darkness.

I believe education is the key to stemming the tide of recidivism. An educated man or woman is a person who has learned to consider all factors before making a decision, thereby minimizing the chances of coming back to prison.

The thing that would help me most with finding work upon my release is a chance, an opportunity. Too many employers find out you have been incarcerated and won’t give you an opportunity. Men and women who have been incarcerated many times make the best employees because they are so grateful for the opportunity to go the extra mile to do a good job. After earning twenty-five cents an hour, how grateful do you think you would be for ten dollars an hour.

When I see the high poverty rate in Rochester what I see are the dashed hopes and dreams of so many young brothers and sisters.

Rochester is a city that festers. As I drive down Jay Street I see hope in the faces of my young brothers, who hope they might see a better day.

When I cruise Chili at night, my fear is the wolves may bite.

By contrast when I go down East Avenue past City Grill everything becomes chill. The colors are lighter, and there are no fights. The pockets are fatter. There are no apes and no King Kongs.

There are doctors, bankers, and lawyers.

I see a city of contradictions.

I see night and day.

I see black and white, and I don’t mean just race.

I see hope and hopelessness.

I see beautiful homes and the homeless

I see what could be and yet will never be

My thoughts to improve life are jobs and education, a wage to enable young people to put down the guns and pick up a pen or a shovel, or a stethoscope. We need to educate our young brothers and sisters that having a baby doesn’t make you grown.

People need to know that as long as a human being has breath a human being is capable of hope. As long as the sun rises in the east and sets in the west, a human being is capable of change. You define who you are.

I, Too, Sing America

I, too, sing America.

Incarcerated.

I found out my younger brother was murdered.

His body left in his car

on Grand Central Parkway,

not far from LaGuardia.

Six shots.

I, too, sing America.

Incarcerated.

I tried to comfort my mom at the funeral home,

as the tears flowed from her eyes for two sons lost,

one to death, one to prison,

while two Correctional Officers sat in the back

to make sure I made no attempt at escape.

I, too, sing America.

Incarcerated.

My fellow inmates and I attended college classes

just past D block in Attica Correctional Facility

debating the merits of Death of A Salesman,

studying Algebra, Geometry,

and Trigonometry.

I, too, sing America.

Incarcerated.

Walking around this crazy yard,

Making plans

to never come back,

but never forgetting

where we came from.

I, too, sing America.

Incarcerated.

Hoping upon hope for an opportunity to show that I belong,

that I can be a contributing member of society,

hoping that my past doesn’t define my future.
I am just looking for a chance, just looking for a chance to say

I, too, am America.

        

My Soul Cries

Every time I hear about another murder in Chicago,

my soul cries.

When I witness the division among people, black and white, Christian and Muslim,

my soul cries.

The richest country on the face of the earth

leaves some its people hungry, sick, and forgotten.

My soul cries.

Young people go to college to pursue American Dream,

but graduate thousands of dollars in debt.

My soul cries.

Dreams deferred.

Dreams unfulfilled.

My soul cries.

Fatherless children in a time when fathers are needed most.

My soul cries.

Young girls bullied and called names

because they don't fit someone else’s definition of beauty.

My soul cries.

When will there be a change?

When will we see one another as people, people?

My soul cries for a time when we are not Democrats or Republicans,

when we are not blacks or white or Hispanics,

for a time when we are Americans.

When will the hate and the distrust stop?

When will be joined together by what we share in common,.

My soul cries for me.

My soul cries for you.

What comes to my mind about Frederick Douglass’s speech, “What To The Slave Is The Fourth of July,” is the speed and magnitude this country has developed monetarily and technologically. The huge skyscrapers that have been erected and the economic resources boggle the mind. Technology is unrivaled in scope and creativity, and yet the rights of human beings move at a snail’s pace. incarceration has skyrocketed. The inner city public school system is deplorable. Black men die in routine traffic stops by enforcement who are supposed to “serve         and protect” citizens.

While the wealth of the wealthy in this country is at an all time high, our poorest citizens are left wondering when will relief come for them.

I cannot help but wonder what Frederick Douglass would make of the progress our country has made post slavery or of the latest research that tells us that school segregation is worse than in 1970.

I live in city that claims to be where Frederick Douglass lived, yet all of his homes are gone save for the one on Hamilton Street. All that remains of where Frederick Douglass lived seems to be parking lots. I wonder why that is. Susan B. Anthony’s residence has been left standing and furnished. Frederick Douglass has parking lots. George Eastman’s house is now the George Eastman Museum. The remains of the homes where Frederick Douglass lives are no more.

I believe it is time for the that the city that claims Frederick Douglass lived here and published The North Star here should invest in a museum dedicated to his life and to his work. His writing and publications provoke conscious thought a century after his death and bear testament to the fact that his life is relevant to all people. Why shouldn’t there be a museum in Rochester to honor and pay respect to this writer, publisher, orator, abolitionist, father, and husband? Until this happens we should remember what Douglass so famously said, “Agitate, agitate, agitate.”

EMMANUEL WILSON

Right now is scary. I think of what this country could look like in the next few years. We may have a Supreme Court that could eliminate our rights. Where do we go from here? Right now I know I need to do all I can as a citizen to join my voice to others to oppose this new order.

I have had to sit on the sidelines and just watch helplessly. I had no say in who became the 45th President of the United States because I was not able to vote. Are the protests and the riots signs of the end of our country?

I believe my teachers in school could have done more, and I could have applied myself more. Teachers should have believed in me and encouraged me to go further. Knowing what I know now I wish I could have gone on to college.

What I would like to learn more about is education helping incarcerated men and women not come back to jail and move forward with their lives.

The justice system is broken, and education would benefit us and lower the recidivism rate. Hopefully college credits will be able to be incorporated into county jails someday.

I would love to look back one day and be able to talk to young adults about not making the same mistake I did when I was younger. I would like incarceration to change lives for the better and instead of making us better criminals make us productive members of society. I believe it would help those incarcerated tremendously to be able to get college credits while incarcerated.

What I have been writing explains a lot about how I feel about the justice system and my education and how the education system and teachers could have pushed me harder like they did other students. I feel like I was cheated. I needed teachers to believe in me.

How I am perceived by my peers means a lot to me because I like to be accepted by everyone. I know that’s not possible. I really don’t know where I belong in the world right now. One thing I do know is that I have a meaning or purpose in the world. What it is I don’t know yet.

The world I live in could be more aware of helping those less fortunate, help all of us to succeed and reach our goals. I want to go to college badly but deep down inside I’m scared. I want to succeed but failure is a big fear for me. It hurts to fail.

JOSEPH EADDY

My thoughts on how I got here today are pretty straightforward. Beware of wolves in sheep’s garments. We, Black people in America today, have been brainwashed so that we hate to see others prosper and succeed. We are set up to hate one another and basically be crabs in a barrel. No matter how hard someone tries to get out, someone is going to pull that person back in.

I tell children the truth. Don’t be like me, be better. I do not have children of my own yet, but I have been a father figure to my nephews for last twenty years.  Every day I motivate them by telling them they are greatness that we come from Kings and Queens, and they can be whatever they want to be. I instructed them to take the word “can’t” out of their vocabulary because I believe when you remove negative thoughts from your mindset you will be able to achieve great things.

Before I was incarcerated I received an Associate’s Degree from Erie Community College in Communication. I transferred to South Carolina State to study finance and economics. I received a football scholarship. That was about ten years ago. I will be going back to school to finish my Bachelor’s Degree in Management Studies with a minor in philosophy after this ordeal is finished.

Before I was employed. I worked in Customer Service for two companies. To most people I made great money, but I wanted more. I have always been tenacious, a leader, and definitely a businessman. This led me to do what I did. Real character is what’s inside me when my back is against the wall. I won’t continue to do what got me put in here.

Incarceration and poverty go hand in hand. The reason most of us are behind bars is because there were not job opportunities like there were back in the days. Kodak’s gone, Xerox is what, General Motors long gone. To think you could graduate from high school and start working the same job until you retired is a dream now a day.

My parents tell me all the time to leave here and never come back. The opportunities in Rochester have dried up. When I leave this place I plan to start my own business and provide opportunities for the community. I, also, plan on volunteering with youth to help them so they don't make the same mistakes I did.

I should have picked whom I called my friends better. I have learned from this experience. I have strengthened my mind, body, and soul.

Rochester, New York is beautiful place filled with so much pain and sorrow. In this great city we call home, I have lost so friends and family members to violence and addiction.

What I see in the city is a struggling infrastructure, one of the weakest school districts in the country, a horrible economy, many deaths, and a mayor who meets with so much opposition. To think that we came from a city that was filled with so much promise and activism to become a murder capital is a sad thing. Frederick Douglass published The North Star here and wrote many of his great pieces on the abolition of slavery here. Susan B. Anthony fought so strongly for the suffrage movement, and George Eastman changed the eye of the beholder. It hurts my heart to see what our City has come to.

We must provide more opportunities, more employment, improve the educational system, and remodel our City. Does the Rochester City School District offer entrepreneur classes? How many classes in trades have been closed down at Edison Tech?

Rochester needs to offer more hope. People need to know our history. People need to know what their ancestors fought for. We need to spread the message of opportunity, not violence.  

I, Too, Sing America

I, too, sing America.

I’m incarcerated.

My physical body is behind bars, but

my mind is free as a bird.

Am I sorry for what I did?

It was a learning experience.

I have learned I need to better myself and to be humble.

People tell me my life is going to be hard because I am a felon,

but when I get out I’m going to hit the ground running.

Life is about learning,

and learning is what I did.

People’s negatives drive me to do more.

Am I not an American?

My people fought wars for this country

and shed much blood, sweat, and tears.

I, too, am an American.

To me the Fourth of July means I will not be celebrating the signing of the Declaration of Independence. I will not be at the family cook out at Genesee Valley Park. I will not be enjoying the beverages and hot dogs. I will be behind these reinforced bars at MCF.

When I was a child

I believed so deeply

in this country,

but

as I got older

my faith

disappeared.

When I was younger I believed that this great country was an equal and free land. That thought vanished away like yesterday.

I used to believe

that all men were created equal

and jail

was full of bad people.

Once I became a teenager I learned that your color and race play a big factor in this “Land of The Free.” I was fifteen when I had my first encounter with the police. They singled me out of a crowd because of the color of my skin. To make matters worse I was well known in the town I grew up in because of my athletic accomplishments. I remember this very profoundly. When the officer took me to my parents’ house, he said “What do your parents do, sell drugs.”

My parents worked very hard for the things they have. My mother was a supervisor at General Motors, and my father was a computer programmer at General Motors. They were very far from drug dealers! My parents taught me that day that we live in two Americas: Black and White. In Black America we are hated because of our skin color, deprived of our heritage and identity, and discouraged from learning the truth. In White America your skin color makes you superior (so-called better than others) and gives you and advantage to be able to get farther ahead in this race called life.

It sickens me that this country we live instills hate into our minds. The truth is that all lives matter, all lives. It is ludicrous that now two hundred and forty-one years later nothing has changed. The racial divide us a bold as ever.
