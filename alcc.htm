The Incarcerated Education Program

"We need to know there is more. We need to know there are other options. We need to know history. We need to see a path to the future. We lack a sense of belief. To accomplish anything, you need first to believe you can. NYSLC is a great example of how to connect with others, discuss topics concerning growth and education, show our families a different part of life, and inspire us to shoot for our dreams."

        N. W., 2016

The New York State Literary Center (NYSLC) cannot change the neighborhoods or economically disadvantaged circumstances that those released from incarceration return to, nor can it change the negative experiences they have been exposed to, but NYSLC can challenge, motivate, and provide tools for the incarcerated to develop a new perspective, a new way of thinking about themselves, their families, and their community. NYSLC offers opportunities that give those incarcerated an enthusiasm to learn, and have been proven to reduce the recidivism rate. NYSLC's goal is to make education relevant, introduce different ideas and perspectives, and to enlarge the view of the community for those incarcerated. 

NYSLC’s experience continues to verify that those with whom we work have had poor educational experiences, lack knowledge of history, see no place for their dreams, and are not able to communicate effectively. They have experienced trauma when they were young, were raised in unstable family structures, and have very little knowledge about the community in which they live. Addressing these is an essential part of NYSLC’s work. Knowing the individuals with whom NYSLC works informs and is the driving force or the organization.

NYSLC’s pedagogy begins with writing and culturally relevant readings that recognize the importance of the student in learning https://www.nyslc.org/reading.htm. Yearly Reading Lists are part of NYSLC’s yearly research.

To have students recognize the importance of history to understand how we experience life today is a vital component of the pedagogy. Discussion with every voice heard and writing in response to the readings fosters respect for each individual and challenges students to think and write about issues that directly affect their lives. The writing is read aloud as part of the process, published, copies shared with all participants, and placed on https://www.nyslc.org/. Dale Davis has developed the writing into theater pieces, https://www.nyslc.org/video1964.htm, https://www.nyslc.org/ReadingJamesBaldwin.htm and https://www.nyslc.org/whatdowedonext.htm are three examples, which have been performed in correctional facilities. The writing has also been the inspiration for participants to work with a visual artist to create murals on the history of Rochester https://www.nyslc.org/rockmural.htm, on the dreams they want to share with their community https://www.nyslc.org/dreamsmural.htm, and on wanting to help parents and children communicate in the visiting rooms of correctional facilities https://www.nyslc.org/freedommural.htm. The murals, along with writing by students, have been exhibited in a gallery https://www.nyslc.org/picturingdreams.htm

and are now housed in Monroe County Jail.

In 2010 writing by incarcerated youth in NYSLC’s Arts, Literary, and the Classroom Community was featured in “Freedom of Expression: An Exhibition of Contemporary Diaries” at The Anne Frank Center in New York. The exhibition of diaries featured contemporary diaries, a facsimile of Anne Frank's original diary, some of the Freedom Writers' diaries, diaries by men and women in prison, and the diaries of those who wrote in NYSLC’s Arts, Literacy, and The Classroom Community https://www.nyslc.org/annefrankabout.htm.

In 2013, NYSLC designed a pilot program, “How to Get From Here To The Rest of The World,” to connect NYSLC’s projects in correctional facility classrooms to the community https://www.nyslc.org/8thyearOverview.htm. The pilot program led to NYSLC identifying the children of those incarcerated as a community need that was not being met. Parents serving sentences do not suffer the consequences alone; children often lose contact with their parents and many times visits are rare. Research has shown NYSLC that children of incarcerated parents are more likely to drop out of school, engage in delinquent behavior, and subsequently be incarcerated themselves. The separation of incarceration causes stress on relationships, emotional and mental strain and financial hardships that can leave a lasting impact on a child. The pilot program led to a newsletter, Food For Thought, https://www.nyslc.org/foodforthought/Food%20For%20Thought%20June%202015.pdf, and Rebuilding Families, a program designed to connect those incarcerated and their families with Rochester’ cultural resources https://www.nyslc.org/RebuildingFamilies.htm

NYSLC continues to provide resources, information, and research that promote education, rehabilitation, community engagement, and rebuild families impacted by incarceration.

Will I be remembered

by someone out there, somewhere,

in some way, some day or

will I fade away?

Will someone shed a tear for me,

or hear my cries, or hear my pleas,

or say a word or two of kindness

to take away my pain just for today

or . . . will I fade away?

Will I miss the rain, the contrast of colors,

my emotions on life’s canvas,

while looking out my window from afar, lying in my bed,

tangled in my sheets,

listening to sad songs playing in my head,

tasting salt within my tears feeding my fears,

thinking to myself

will I fade away?

        D. D. R., 2019

Narratives of Our Life and Times

Narrative of Our Life and Times is a NYSLC COMMUNITY ENGAGEMENT project with incarcerated youth. It was designed and developed by NYSLC as a means for incarcerated youth to reach out and share their thoughts and observations on their neighborhoods and their community. Narrative of Our Life and Times was made possible with funding from the Guido and Ellen Palma Foundation, the Rochester City School District, and the New York State Council on the Arts. The New York State Literary Center thanks the City of Rochester; Rochester Public Library, Local History Division; and the Albert R. Stone Negative Collection, Rochester Museum and Science Center for permission to use the photographs.
